QR Decomposition and Least Squares Problem

Posted on 2021-10-28 Edited on 2021-11-02 In Mathematics / Numerical linear algebra
Not done.

QR Decomposition

For any \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}\), there exists a decomposition \[A=QR,\] where \(Q \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}\) is orthonormal, and \(R \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\) is upper triangular.

QR via Gram-Schmidt

Given matrix \(A=\begin{bmatrix} & \\ \mathbf{a}_1 & \cdots & \mathbf{a}_n \\ & \end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}\) (assume \(\text{rank}(A)=n\)), we want to find an orthonormal matrix \(\begin{bmatrix} & \\ \mathbf{q}_1 & \cdots & \mathbf{q}_n \\ & \end{bmatrix}\) s.t. \(\text{Span}(\mathbf{q_1}, \cdots, \mathbf{q}_n)=\text{Span}(\mathbf{a}_1, \cdots, \mathbf{a}_n)\).

The Gram-Schmidt algorithm performs the following: \[\begin{aligned} \mathbf{q}_1&=\frac{\mathbf{a}_1}{\|\mathbf{a}_1\|_2} \\ \widetilde{\mathbf{q}}_2&=\mathbf{a}_2-\mathbf{q}_1\mathbf{q}_1^T\mathbf{a}_2 \\ \mathbf{q}_2&=\frac{\widetilde{\mathbf{q}}_2}{\|\mathbf{q}_2\|_2} \\ &\ \ \vdots \\ \widetilde{\mathbf{q}}_j&=\mathbf{a}_j-\sum_{i=1}^{j-1} \mathbf{q}_i\mathbf{q}_i^T\mathbf{a}_j \\ \mathbf{q}_j&=\frac{\widetilde{\mathbf{q}}_j}{\|\mathbf{q}_j\|_2} \end{aligned}\]

Gram-Schmidt gives a QR factorization. Let \(r_{ij}=\mathbf{q}_i^T\mathbf{a}_j\), \(i \neq j\), and \(\displaystyle r_{jj}=\left\|\mathbf{a}_j-\sum_{i=1}^{j-1} r_{ij}\mathbf{q}_i\right\|_2\). Then \[\begin{aligned} \mathbf{a}_1&=r_{11}\mathbf{q}_1 \\ \mathbf{a}_2&=r_{12}\mathbf{q}_1+r_{22}\mathbf{q}_2 \\ &\ \ \vdots \\ \mathbf{a}_j&=r_{1j}\mathbf{q}_1+r_{2j}\mathbf{q}_2+\cdots+r_{jj}\mathbf{q}_j \end{aligned}\] which in turn is \(A=QR\), where \(Q^TQ=I_n\) and \(R\) is upper triangular.

Gram-Schmidt is not the recommended way to compute the QR factorization, as it is numerically unstable.

Householder QR Factorization

Householder Reflector

Householder reflector is the class of matrices \(H_\mathbf{v}\) that are symmetric, orthogonal, and can be written as a rank one update of the identity \[H_\mathbf{v}=I-2\mathbf{v}\mathbf{v}^T, \|\mathbf{v}\|_2=1.\] Note that if \(\mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{v}=0\), then \(H_\mathbf{v}\mathbf{w}=\mathbf{w}\).

  • \(H\) is symmetric, as \(H^T=(I-2\mathbf{v}\mathbf{v}^T)^T=I^T-2\mathbf{v}\mathbf{v}^T=I-2\mathbf{v}\mathbf{v}^T=H\).

  • \(H\) is orthogonal, as \(H^TH=HH^T=H^2=(I-2\mathbf{v}\mathbf{v}^T)(I-2\mathbf{v}\mathbf{v}^T)=I-4\mathbf{v}\mathbf{v}^T+4\mathbf{v}\mathbf{v}^T\mathbf{v}\mathbf{v}^T=I\).

  • The eigenvalues of \(H\) are \(1\) (\(n-1\) copies) and \(-1\) (\(1\) copy).

  • Given \(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^n\) s.t. \(\|\mathbf{u}\|_2=\|\mathbf{w}\|_2\) and \(\mathbf{u} \neq \mathbf{w}\), \(H_\mathbf{v}=I-2\mathbf{v}\mathbf{v}^T\) with \(\displaystyle \mathbf{v}=\frac{\mathbf{w}-\mathbf{u}}{\|\mathbf{w}-\mathbf{u}\|_2}\) gives \(H_\mathbf{v}\mathbf{u}=\mathbf{w} \Leftrightarrow \mathbf{u}=H_\mathbf{v}\mathbf{w}\) (and thus reflector).

Proof. Suppose w.l.o.g. that \(\|\mathbf{u}\|_2=\|\mathbf{w}\|_2=1\), then \[\begin{aligned} H_\mathbf{v}\mathbf{u}&=\mathbf{u}-2\frac{(\mathbf{w}-\mathbf{u})(\mathbf{w}-\mathbf{u})^T}{\|\mathbf{w}-\mathbf{u}\|_2^2}\mathbf{u} \\&=\mathbf{u}-\frac{2(\mathbf{w}-\mathbf{u})^T\mathbf{u}}{\|\mathbf{w}-\mathbf{u}\|_2^2}(\mathbf{w}-\mathbf{u}) \\&=\left(1+\frac{2(\mathbf{w}-\mathbf{u})^T\mathbf{u}}{2(1-\mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{u})}\right)\mathbf{u}-\frac{2(\mathbf{w}-\mathbf{u})^T\mathbf{u}}{2(1-\mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{u})}\mathbf{w} \\&=\left(1+\frac{2(\mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{u}-1)}{2(1-\mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{u})}\right)\mathbf{u}-\frac{2(\mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{u}-1)}{2(1-\mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{u})}\mathbf{w} \\&=\mathbf{w}. \end{aligned}\]

\(\square\)

By choosing the \(\mathbf{v}\) appropriately, one can perform a variety of operations to a given \(\mathbf{x}\). A primary example is that \[\mathbf{v}=\frac{\mathbf{x}-\|\mathbf{x}\|\mathbf{e}_1}{\|\mathbf{x}-\|\mathbf{x}\|\mathbf{e}_1\|}\] which satisfies \(H\mathbf{x}=\begin{bmatrix} \|\mathbf{x}\| & 0 & \cdots & 0 \end{bmatrix}^T\), i.e., an arbitrary vector \(\mathbf{x}\) is mapped by \(H\) to a multiple of \(\mathbf{e}_1\).

Householder QR Factorization

The first step to obtain QR is to find \(H_1\) s.t. \(H_1\mathbf{a_1}=\begin{bmatrix} \|\mathbf{a}_1\|_2 \\ 0 \\ \vdots \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\) and repeat to get \(H_n \cdots H_2H_1A=R\) (upper triangular), then \(A=(H_1 \cdots H_{n-1}H_n)R=QR\).

We present a pictorial illustration. Suppose \(A=\begin{bmatrix} * & * & * & * \\ * & * & * & * \\ * & * & * & * \\ * & * & * & * \\ * & * & * & * \end{bmatrix}\). We apply a sequence of Householder reflectors. First, \[H_1A=(I-2\mathbf{v}_1\mathbf{v}_1^T)A=\begin{bmatrix} \|\mathbf{a}_1\|_2 & \bullet & \bullet & \bullet \\ 0 & \bullet & \bullet & \bullet \\ 0 & \bullet & \bullet & \bullet \\ 0 & \bullet & \bullet & \bullet \\ 0 & \bullet & \bullet & \bullet \end{bmatrix},\ \text{where}\ H_1\mathbf{a}_1=\begin{bmatrix} \|\mathbf{a}_1\|_2 \\ 0 \\ \vdots \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}.\]

Then, \[H_2H_1A=(I-2\mathbf{v}_2\mathbf{v}_2^T)H_1A=\begin{bmatrix} \|\mathbf{a}_1\|_2 & \bullet & \bullet & \bullet \\ 0 & \|\widetilde{\mathbf{a}}_2\|_2 & \square & \square \\ 0 & 0 & \square & \square \\ 0 & 0 & \square & \square \\ 0 & 0 & \square & \square \\ \end{bmatrix},\] where we need \(\mathbf{v}_2=\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ * \\ \vdots \\ * \end{bmatrix}\) so that \(H_2=\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & \cdots & 0\\ 0 \\ \vdots \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\) does not affect the first row and column of \(H_1A\). We can choose \(\mathbf{u}=\begin{bmatrix} \bullet \\ \|\widetilde{\mathbf{a}}_2\|_2 \\ 0 \\ \vdots \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(\mathbf{w}=\widetilde{\mathbf{a}}_2\), where \(H_2\widetilde{\mathbf{a}}_2=\mathbf{u}\).

Similarly, \[H_n \cdots H_3H_2H_1A=\begin{bmatrix} \|\mathbf{a}_1\|_2 & \bullet & \bullet & \bullet \\ 0 & \|\widetilde{\mathbf{a}}_2\|_2 & \square & \square \\ 0 & 0 & \|\widehat{\mathbf{a}}_3\|_2 & \triangle \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \|\dot{\mathbf{a}}_4\|_2 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ \end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix} R \\ O \end{bmatrix}.\] Then \[A=(H_1^T \cdots H_{n-1}^TH_n^T)\begin{bmatrix} R \\ O \end{bmatrix}=:Q_F\begin{bmatrix} R \\ O \end{bmatrix},\] which is a full QR, where \(Q_F\) is square orthogonal. We can write \(Q_f=\begin{bmatrix} Q & Q_\perp \end{bmatrix}\), where \(Q \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}\) is orthonormal, then \(A=QR\) is the QR factorization.

  • The cost for Householder QR is \(\displaystyle \frac{4}{3}n^3\) flops (twice that of LU).

  • Householder QR is unconditionally backward stable: the computed version satisfies \(\widehat{Q}\widehat{R}=A+\Delta A\) and \(\|\widehat{Q}^T\widehat{Q}-I\|_2=\varepsilon\).

  • The algorithm gives a constructive proof for \(A=QR\) existence.

  • To solve \(A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}\), we can solve \(R\mathbf{x}=Q^T\mathbf{b}\) via triangle solve. But the cost is double compared to that for LU (rarely used).

Givens Rotation

Each Householder reflector acts globally and it affects all the entries of the matrix. For structured matrix, such as sparse matrix, sometimes there is a better tool to reduce the matrix to triangular form (and other forms) by working more locally. Givens rotation gives a convenient tool for this.

Given rotation is matrix of the form \[G=\begin{bmatrix} c & s \\ -s & c \end{bmatrix}, c^2+s^2=1.\] Note that \(G^TG=I_2\), but \(G\) is not symmetric.

For example to compute the QR factorization for an upper Hessenberg matrix, one can perform \[A=\begin{bmatrix} * & * & * & * & * \\ * & * & * & * & * \\ & * & * & * & * \\ & & * & * & * \\ & & & * & * \\ \end{bmatrix}, G_1A=\begin{bmatrix} * & * & * & * & * \\ & * & * & * & * \\ & * & * & * & * \\ & & * & * & * \\ & & & * & * \\ \end{bmatrix}, G_2G_1A=\begin{bmatrix} * & * & * & * & * \\ & * & * & * & * \\ & & * & * & * \\ & & * & * & * \\ & & & * & * \\ \end{bmatrix},\] \[G_3G_2G_1A=\begin{bmatrix} * & * & * & * & * \\ & * & * & * & * \\ & & * & * & * \\ & & & * & * \\ & & & * & * \\ \end{bmatrix}, G_4G_3G_2G_1A=G_3G_2G_1A=\begin{bmatrix} * & * & * & * & * \\ & * & * & * & * \\ & & * & * & * \\ & & & * & * \\ & & & & * \\ \end{bmatrix}=:R.\] Hence, \(A=G_1^TG_2^TG_3^TG_4^TR\) is the QR factorization.

  • \(G\) acts locally on two rows.

  • Non-neighboring rows or columns are allowed. For example, a rotation acting on the \(i\)th and \(j\)th columns will have \(c\) and \(s\) values in the \((i, i)\), \((i, j)\), \((j, i)\) and \((j, j)\) entries.

Least Squares Problem

QR Solution

Given \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}\) (\(m \geq n\)), and \(\mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^m\), a least squares problem seeks to find \(\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n\) s.t. the residual is minimized: \[\min_\mathbf{x} \|A\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{b}\|_2.\]

  • It is an overdetermined linear system, and \(A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}\) is usually impossible. Thus, the we minimize \(\|A\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{b}\|\).

  • We assume full rank, i.e., \(\text{rank}(A)=n\), which makes solution unique.

Theorem 6.1    Let \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}\) (\(m>n\)), and \(\mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^m\), with \(\text{rank}(A)=n\). The least suqares problem \(\displaystyle \min_\mathbf{x} \|A\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{b}\|_2\) has a solution given by \(\mathbf{x}=R^{-1}Q^T\mathbf{b}\), where \(A=QR\) is the QR factorization.

Proof. Let \(A=\begin{bmatrix} Q & Q_\perp \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} R \\ O \end{bmatrix}=Q_F\begin{bmatrix} R \\ O \end{bmatrix}\) be full QR factorization. Then \[\|A\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{b}\|_2=\left\|\begin{bmatrix} Q & Q_\perp \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} R \\ O \end{bmatrix}\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{b}\right\|_2=\left\|\begin{bmatrix} R \\ O \end{bmatrix}\mathbf{x}-\begin{bmatrix} Q^T\mathbf{b} \\ Q^T_\perp\mathbf{b} \end{bmatrix}\right\|_2=\left\|\begin{bmatrix} R\mathbf{x}-Q^T\mathbf{b} \\ -Q^T_\perp\mathbf{b} \end{bmatrix}\right\|_2.\] Then \(\|A\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{b}\|_2\) is minimized when \(R\mathbf{x}-Q^T\mathbf{b}\), i.e., \(\mathbf{x}=R^{-1}Q^T\mathbf{b}\).

\(\square\)

The algorithm is obvious: We compute the QR factorization \(A=QR\) (using Householder QR), and then solve linear system \(R\mathbf{x}=Q^T\mathbf{b}\).

Normal Equation Solution

To solve the least squares problem, \(\mathbf{x}=R^{-1}Q^T\mathbf{b}\) is the solution iff \(\mathbf{x}\) is the solution for \(n \times n\) normal equation \((A^T)\mathbf{x}=A^T\mathbf{b}\). Note that \(A^TA \succeq 0\). \(A^TA \succ 0\) if \(\text{rank}(A)=n\), and thus we can use Cholesky factorization to solve, i.e., \(A^TA=R^TR\). However, the method is not backward stable.

Application: Regression and Function Approximation

Given function \(f: [-1, 1] \to \mathbb{R}\), we consider approximating via polynomial \[f(x) \approx p(x)=\sum_{i=0} c_ix^i.\] A very common technique is regression: We sample \(f\) at points \(\{z_i\}_{i=1}^m\), and find coefficients \(\mathbf{c}\) defined by Vandermonde system \(A\mathbf{c} \approx \mathbf{f}\): \[\begin{bmatrix} 1 & z_1 & \cdots & z_1^n \\ 1 & z_2 & \cdots & z_2^n \\ \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots \\ 1 & z_m & \cdots & z_m^n \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} c_0 \\ \vdots \\ c_n \end{bmatrix} \approx \begin{bmatrix} f(z_1) \\ f(z_2) \\ \vdots \\ f(z_m) \end{bmatrix}.\]
