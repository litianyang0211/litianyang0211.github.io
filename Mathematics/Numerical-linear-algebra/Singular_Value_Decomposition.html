Singular Value Decomposition

Posted on 2021-10-10 In Mathematics / Numerical linear algebra
We introduce the singular value decomposition (SVD) and give a proof. Next, we talk about the application of SVD and the connection between SVD and eigenvalue decomposition. Finally, we explore the uniqueness of SVD.

Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)

  • Symmetric eigenvalue decomposition: \(S=V\Lambda V^T\) for symmetric \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\), where \(V^TV=I_n\) (i.e., \(V\) is an orthogonal matrix), and \(\Lambda=\text{diag}(\lambda_1, \cdots, \lambda_n)\), \(\lambda_i \in \mathbb{R}\).

  • Singular value decomposition (SVD): \(A=U\Sigma V^T\) for any \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}\) assuming \(m \geq n\) for definiteness, where \(U^TU=V^TV=I_n\), and \(\Sigma=\text{diag}(\sigma_1, \cdots, \sigma_n)\), \(\sigma_1 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_n \geq 0\). \(\sigma_i\) is called the singular value of \(A\), the column of \(U\) is called the left singular vector, and the column of \(V\) is called the right singular vector.

\(UU^T \neq I_m\) if \(m>n\). But \(VV^T=I_n\) since \(V\) is square.

Proof. Take Gram matrix \(A^TA\), which is symmetric, then its eigndecomposition is \(A^TA=V\Lambda V^T\), where \(\lambda_1 \geq \cdots \geq \lambda_n \geq 0\). Define \(B=AV\), then \[B^TB=V^TA^TAV=V^TV\Lambda V^TV=\Lambda.\] Since \((B^TB)_{ii}=\mathbf{b}_i^T\mathbf{b}_i=\|\mathbf{b}_i\|_2^2 \geq 0\), where \(\mathbf{b}_i\) is the \(i\)th column of \(B\), then \(B^TB=\Lambda \succeq 0\). Then \[\begin{aligned} B&=\begin{bmatrix} & \\ \mathbf{u}_1 & \cdots & \mathbf{u}_r & \mathbf{0} & \cdots & \mathbf{0} \\ & \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} \sigma_1 \\ & \ddots \\ & & \sigma_r \\ & & & 0 \\ & & & & \ddots \\ & & & & & 0 \end{bmatrix} \\&=\begin{bmatrix} & \\ \mathbf{u}_1 & \cdots & \mathbf{u}_r & \mathbf{u}_{r+1} & \cdots & \mathbf{u}_n \\ & \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} \sigma_1 \\ & \ddots \\ & & \sigma_r \\ & & & 0 \\ & & & & \ddots \\ & & & & & 0 \end{bmatrix} \\&=U\Sigma, \end{aligned}\] where U contains orthonormal vectors, and \(\sigma_i=\sqrt{\lambda_i}\). Since \(B=AV\), then \(A=BV^T=U\Sigma V^T\).

\(\square\)

  • Full SVD: \(A=U\begin{bmatrix} \Sigma \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}V^T\), where \(U \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times m}\) is orthogonal. See MORE.

  • If \(A \in \mathbb{C}^{m \times n}\), then the SVD of \(A\) is \(A=U\Sigma V^*\), where \(U^*U=V^*V=I_n=VV^*\).

Application

From the SVD, we know:

  • Rank \(r\) of \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}\): Number of nonzero singular values \(\sigma_i(A)\).

  • Column space (linear subspace spanned by vectors \(A\mathbf{x}\)): Span of \(U=[\mathbf{u}_1, \cdots, \mathbf{u}_r]\), i.e., \[\text{Span}(\text{col}(A))=\text{Span}(\mathbf{u}_1, \cdots, \mathbf{u}_r).\]

  • Row space: Row span of \(\mathbf{v}_1^T, \cdots, \mathbf{v}_r^T\).

  • Null space: Span of \(\mathbf{v}_{r+1}, \cdots, \mathbf{v}_n\).

SVD and Eigenvalue Decomposition

  • \(V\) contains eigenvectors of \(A^TA\).

  • \(U\) contains eigenvectors (for nonzero eigenvalues) of \(AA^T\) (up to sign).

  • \(\sigma_i=\sqrt{\lambda_i(A^TA)}\).

  • Jordan-Wieldant matrix \(M=\begin{bmatrix} 0 & A \\ A^T & 0 \end{bmatrix}\): \(M\) has eigenvalues \(\pm\sigma_i(A)\), and \(m-n\) copies of eigenvalues at \(0\). Its eigenvector matrix is \(\begin{bmatrix} U & U & U_0 \\ V & -V & 0 \end{bmatrix}\), where \(A^TU_0=0\), \(U_0\) is empty when \(m=n\).

    • \(M\) along with the Gram matrix \(A^TA\), is a useful tool when we try to extend a result on symmetric eigenvalues to an analogue in terms of the SVD (or vice versa).

Uniqueness

  • The singular vectors are not uniquely determined.

    • The singular vectors can be flipped in signs. For example, \[A=U\Sigma V^T=(US_1)\Sigma(S_1V^T),\] where \(S_1=\begin{bmatrix} \pm1 \\ & \ddots \\ & & \pm 1 \end{bmatrix}\), is a valid SVD.

    • If singular values are distinct, then the sign is the only degree of freedom.

    • If singular values are not distinct (i.e., \(\sigma_i=\sigma_{i+1}\) for some \(i\)), then there is a higher degree of freedom in the SVD.

    • The singular values are always unique, as they are the eigenvalues of the Gram matrix.
