Linear Algebra

Posted on 2021-10-09 Edited on 2021-10-12 In Mathematics / Numerical linear algebra
A quick review of linear algebra.

Structured Matrix

For square matrix:

  • Symmetric: \(A_{ij}=A_{ji}\) (Hermitian: \(A_{ij}=\overline{A_{ji}}\) in \(\mathbb{C}^{n \times n}\), where \(\overline{A_{ji}}\) means complex conjugate).

    • Symmetric positive definite (SPD): \(A \succ 0\).

    • Symmetric positive semidefinite (SPSD): \(A \succeq 0\).

  • Orthogonal: \(AA^T=A^TA=I\) (Unitary: \(AA^*=A^*A=I\) in \(\mathbb{C}^{n \times n}\), where \((A^*)_{ij}=\overline{A_{ji}}\)).

  • Skew-symmetric: \(A_{ij}=-A_{ji}\) (Skew-Hermitian: \(A_{ij}=-\overline{A_{ji}}\) in \(\mathbb{C}^{n \times n}\)).

  • Normal: \(A^TA=AA^T\).

  • Tridiagonal: \(A_{ij}=0\) if \(|i-j|>1\).

  • Upper triangular: \(A_{ij}=0\) if \(i>j\).

For matrix \(A \in \mathbb{C}^{m \times n}\), \(m \geq n\):

  • Hessenberg: \(A_{ij}=0\) if \(i>j+1\).

  • Matrix with orthonormal columns: \(A^*A=I_n\). Note that \(AA^* \neq I_m\) if \(m>n\).

  • Sparse: Most elements are zero.

Vector Norm

For vectors \(\mathbf{x}=[x_1, \cdots, x_n]^T \in \mathbb{C}^n\):

  • \(\mathscr{l}^p\) norm: \(\|\mathbf{x}\|_p=(|x_1|^p+\cdots+|x_n|^p)^{1/p}\).

    • Euclidean norm or \(\mathscr{l}^2\) norm: \(\|\mathbf{x}\|_2=\sqrt{|x_1|^2+\cdots+|x_n|^2}\).

    • \(\mathscr{l}^1\) norm: \(\|\mathbf{x}\|_1=|x_1|+\cdots+|x_n|\).

    • \(\mathscr{l}^\infty\) norm: \(\displaystyle \|\mathbf{x}\|_\infty=\max_i |x_i|\).

Norm Axioms

  • \(\|\alpha\mathbf{x}\|=|\alpha|\|\mathbf{x}\|\) for any \(\alpha \in \mathbb{C}\).

  • \(\|\mathbf{x}\| \geq 0\) and \(\|\mathbf{x}\|=0 \Leftrightarrow \mathbf{x}=\mathbf{0}\).

  • \(\|\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{y}\| \leq \|\mathbf{x}\|+\|\mathbf{y}\|\).

Inequality

  • \(\displaystyle \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\|\mathbf{x}\|_2 \leq \|\mathbf{x}\|_\infty \leq \|\mathbf{x}\|_2\).
Proof. Suppose \(\displaystyle \max_i |x_i|=|x_k|\). We have \[\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\|\mathbf{x}\|_2=\sqrt{\frac{|x_1|^2+\cdots+|x_n|^2}{n}} \leq \sqrt{\frac{n|x_k|^2}{n}}=|x_k|=\|\mathbf{x}\|_\infty.\] Since \(|x_k|^2 \leq |x_1|^2+\cdots+|x_k|^2+\cdots+|x_n|^2\), then \[\|\mathbf{x}\|_\infty=|x_k|=\sqrt{|x_k|^2} \leq \sqrt{|x_1|^2+\cdots+|x_n|^2}=\|\mathbf{x}\|_2.\]

\(\square\)

  • \(\displaystyle \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\|\mathbf{x}\|_1 \leq \|\mathbf{x}\|_2 \leq \|\mathbf{x}\|_1\).

Proof. We have \[0 \leq \sum_{i \neq j}(|x_i|-|x_j|)^2=(n-1)\sum_{i=1}^n |x_i|^2-2\sum_{i \neq j} |x_i||x_j|.\] Then \[\sum_{i=1}^n |x_i|^2+2\sum_{i \neq j} |x_i||x_j| \leq n\sum_{i=1}^n |x_i|^2,\] i.e., \[\left(\sum_{i=1}^n |x_i|\right)^2 \leq n \sum_{i=1}^n |x_i|^2.\] Hence, \[\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\|\mathbf{x}\|_1=\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} \sum_{i=1}^n |x_i| \leq \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n |x_i|^2}=\|\mathbf{x}\|_2.\]

Besides \[\|\mathbf{x}\|_2=\sqrt{|x_1|^2+\cdots+|x_n|^2} \leq \sqrt{|x_1|^2}+\cdots+\sqrt{|x_n|^2}=|x_1|+\cdots+|x_n|=\|\mathbf{x}\|_1.\]

\(\square\)

  • \(\displaystyle \frac{1}{n}\|\mathbf{x}\|_1 \leq \|\mathbf{x}\|_\infty \leq \|\mathbf{x}\|_1\).
Proof. Suppose \(\displaystyle \max_i |x_i|=|x_k|\). We have \[\frac{1}{n}\|\mathbf{x}\|_1=\frac{|x_1|+\cdots+|x_n|}{n} \leq \frac{n|x_k|}{n}=|x_k|=\|\mathbf{x}\|_\infty \leq |x_1|+\cdots+|x_k|+\cdots+|x_n|=\|\mathbf{x}\|_1.\]

\(\square\)

  • (Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality) \(|\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{y}| \leq \|\mathbf{x}\|_2\|\mathbf{y}\|_2\).
Proof. Let \(M=\begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{x}^T \\ \mathbf{y}^T \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} & \\ \mathbf{x} & \mathbf{y} \\ & \end{bmatrix}\), we know \(M\) is symmetric and positive semidefinite, i.e., \(\left|\begin{matrix} \mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{x} & \mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{y} \\ \mathbf{y}^T\mathbf{x} & \mathbf{y}^T\mathbf{y} \end{matrix}\right| \geq 0\). Then \(\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{x}\mathbf{y}^T\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{y}\mathbf{y}^T\mathbf{x} \geq 0\), i.e., \(\|\mathbf{x}\|_2^2\|\mathbf{y}\|_2^2 \geq (\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{y})^2\), and thus \(\|\mathbf{x}\|_2\|\mathbf{y}\|_2 \geq |\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{y}|\).

\(\square\)

Unitarily Invariant

  • \(\|\cdot\|_2\) is unitarily invariant as \(\|U\mathbf{x}\|_2=\|\mathbf{x}\|_2\) for any unitary \(U\) and an \(\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{C}^n\).
Proof. We have \[\|U\mathbf{x}\|_2^2=(U\mathbf{x})^*(U\mathbf{x})=\mathbf{x}^*U^*U\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{x}^*I\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{x}^*\mathbf{x}=\|\mathbf{x}\|_2^2.\]

\(\square\)

Matrix Norm

For matrix \(A \in \mathbb{C}^{m \times n}\):

  • \(\mathscr{l}^p\) norm: \(\displaystyle \|A\|_p=\max_\mathbf{x} \frac{\|A\mathbf{x}\|_p}{\|\mathbf{x}\|_p}=\max_{\|\mathbf{x}\|_p=1} \|A\mathbf{x}\|_p\).

    • \(\mathscr{l}^2\) norm or spectral norm or operator norm: \(\|A\|_2=\sigma_\max(A)\) (i.e., the largest singular value).

    • \(\mathscr{l}^1\) norm: \(\displaystyle \|A\|_1=\max_i \sum_{j=1}^m |A_{ji}|\).

    • \(\mathscr{l}^\infty\) norm: \(\displaystyle \|A\|_\infty=\max_i \sum_{j=1}^n |A_{ij}|\).

Proof. Suppose \(p=2\), then \[\|A\|_2=\max_{\|\mathbf{x}\|_2=1} \|A\mathbf{x}\|_2.\] For any \(\mathbf{x}\) with unit norm \(\|\mathbf{x}\|_2=1\), we have \[\|A\mathbf{x}\|_2=\|U\Sigma V^T\mathbf{x}\|_2=\|\Sigma V^T\mathbf{x}\|_2=\|\Sigma\mathbf{y}\|_2,\] where \(\|\mathbf{y}\|_2=1\). Then \[\|A\mathbf{x}\|_2=\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n \sigma_i^2y_i^2} \leq \sqrt{\sigma_1^2\sum_{i=1}^n y_i^2}=\sigma_1.\] Take \(\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{v}_1\), then \[\|A\mathbf{x}\|_2=\|U\Sigma V^T\mathbf{v}_1\|_2=\|U\Sigma\mathbf{e}_1\|_2=\|\sigma_1\mathbf{u}_1\|_2=\sigma_1.\] Therefore, \[\|A\|_2=\max_{\|\mathbf{x}\|_2=1} \|A\mathbf{x}\|_2=\sigma_1=\sigma_\max(A).\]

Suppose \(p=1\). Let the columns of \(A\) be \(\mathbf{a}_1, \cdots, \mathbf{a}_n\) and \(\displaystyle \max_{i}\|\mathbf{a}_i\|_1=\|\mathbf{a}_k\|_1\), then \[\begin{aligned} \|A\|_1&=\max_{\|\mathbf{x}\|_1=1} \|A\mathbf{x}\|_1=\max_{\|\mathbf{x}\|_1=1} \left\|x_1\mathbf{a}_1+\cdots+x_n\mathbf{a}_n\right\|_1 \\&\leq \max_{\|\mathbf{x}\|_1=1} (|x_1|\|\mathbf{a}_1\|_1+\cdots+|x_n|\|\mathbf{a}_n\|_1) \\&\leq \max_{\|\mathbf{x}\|_1=1} (|x_1|+\cdots+|x_n|)\|\mathbf{a}_k\|_1 \\&=\|\mathbf{a}_k\|_1, \end{aligned}\] i.e., the largest \(\mathscr{l}^1\) norm of the columns of \(A\), which is \(\displaystyle \max_i \sum_{j=1}^m |A_{ji}|\).

Suppose \(p=\infty\). Let the rows of \(A\) be \(\mathbf{b}_1, \cdots, \mathbf{b}_m\), then \[\begin{aligned} \|A\|_\infty&=\max_{\|\mathbf{x}\|_\infty=1} \|A\mathbf{x}\|_\infty=\max_{\|\mathbf{x}\|_\infty=1} \max_i |b_{i1}x_1+\cdots+b_{in}x_n| \\&\leq \max_{\|\mathbf{x}\|_\infty=1} \max_i (|b_{i1}||x_1|+\cdots+|b_{in}||x_n|) \\&\leq \max_{\|\mathbf{x}\|_\infty=1} \max_i (|b_{i1}|+\cdots+|b_{in}|) \\&=\max_i (|b_{i1}|+\cdots+|b_{in}|), \end{aligned}\] i.e., the largest \(\mathscr{l}^1\) norm of the rows of \(A\), which is \(\displaystyle \max_i \sum_{j=1}^n |A_{ij}|\).

\(\square\)

  • Frobenius norm: \(\displaystyle \|A\|_F=\sqrt{\sum_i \sum_j |A_{ij}|^2}=\sqrt{\text{Tr}(A^*A)}=\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n (\sigma_i(A))^2}\).

Proof. We have \[\sqrt{\text{Tr}(A^*A)}=\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n (A^*A)_{ii}}=\sqrt{\sum_i \sum_j |A_{ij}|^2}.\]

We now consider \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}\). By SVD, \(\|A\|_F=\|U\Sigma V\|_F\), where \(U\) and \(V\) are orthogonal. Since \(\|\cdot\|_F\) is unitarily invariant, then \[\|A\|_F=\|U\Sigma V\|_F=\|U^T(U\Sigma V)V^T\|_F=\|\Sigma\|_F=\sqrt{\sum_i \sum_j |\Sigma_{ij}|^2}=\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n (\sigma_i(A))^2}.\]

\(\square\)

  • Trace norm or nuclear norm: \(\displaystyle \|A\|_*=\sum_{i=1}^{\min(m, n)} \sigma_i(A)\).

Norm Axioms

  • \(\|\alpha A\|=|\alpha|\|A\|\) for any \(\alpha \in \mathbb{C}\).

  • \(\|A\| \geq 0\) and \(\|A\|=0 \Leftrightarrow A=O\).

  • \(\|A+B\| \leq \|A\|+\|B\|\).

Inequality

  • \(\displaystyle \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\|A\|_\infty \leq \|A\|_2 \leq \sqrt{m}\|A\|_\infty\).

Proof. We have \[\|A\mathbf{x}\|_\infty \leq \|A\mathbf{x}\|_2 \leq \|A\|_2\|\mathbf{x}\|_2 \leq \|A\|_2\sqrt{n}\|\mathbf{x}\|_\infty,\] then \[\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\frac{\|A\mathbf{x}\|_\infty}{\|\mathbf{x}\|_\infty} \leq \|A\|_2.\] Since \(\mathbf{x}\) is arbitrary, then \[\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\|A\|_\infty \leq \|A\|_2.\]

Besides, \[\frac{1}{\sqrt{m}}\|A\mathbf{x}\|_2 \leq \|A\mathbf{x}\|_\infty \leq \|A\|_\infty\|\mathbf{x}\|_\infty \leq \|A\|_\infty\|\mathbf{x}\|_2,\] then \[\frac{\|A\mathbf{x}\|_2}{\|\mathbf{x}\|_2} \leq \sqrt{m}\|A\|_\infty.\] Since \(\mathbf{x}\) is arbitrary, then \[\|A\|_2 \leq \sqrt{m}\|A\|_\infty.\]

\(\square\)

  • \(\displaystyle \frac{1}{\sqrt{m}}\|A\|_1 \leq \|A\|_2 \leq \sqrt{n}\|A\|_1\).

Proof. We have \[\frac{1}{\sqrt{m}}\|A\mathbf{x}\|_1 \leq \|A\mathbf{x}\|_2 \leq \|A\|_2\|\mathbf{x}\|_2 \leq \|A\|_2\|\mathbf{x}\|_1.\] Since \(\mathbf{x}\) is arbitrary, then \[\frac{1}{\sqrt{m}}\|A\|_1 \leq \|A\|_2.\]

Besides, \[\|A\mathbf{x}\|_2 \leq \|A\mathbf{x}\|_1 \leq \|A\|_1\|\mathbf{x}\|_1 \leq \|A\|_1\sqrt{n}\|\mathbf{x}\|_2.\] Since \(\mathbf{x}\) is arbitrary, then \[\|A\|_2 \leq \sqrt{n}\|A\|_1.\]

\(\square\)

  • \(\|A\|_2 \leq \|A\|_F \leq \sqrt{\min(m, n)}\|A\|_2\).
Proof. Recall that the \((\sigma_i(A))^2\) is the eigenvalues of \(A^*A\), and \[\text{Tr}(A^*A)=\sum_{i=1}^{\min(m, n)} \lambda_i(A^*A).\] Hence, \[\|A\|_2=\sigma_\max(A)=\sqrt{\lambda_\max(A^*A)} \leq \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{\min(m, n)} \lambda_i(A^*A)}=\sqrt{\text{Tr}(A^*A)}=\|A\|_F.\] Besides, \[\|A\|_F=\sqrt{\text{Tr}(A^*A)}=\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{\min(m, n)} \lambda_i(A^*A)} \leq \sqrt{\min(m, n) \lambda_\max(A^*A)}=\sqrt{\min(m, n)}\|A\|_2.\]

\(\square\)

Unitarily Invariant

  • \(\|\cdot\|_2\), \(\|\cdot\|_F\) and \(\|\cdot\|_*\) are unitarily invariant as \(\|A\|=\|UAV\|\) for any unitary (or orthogonal) \(U\), \(V\).

Proof. We have \[\|UAV\|_2=\max_\mathbf{x} \frac{\|U(AV\mathbf{x})\|_2}{\|\mathbf{x}\|_2}=\max_\mathbf{x} \frac{\|AV\mathbf{x}\|_2}{\|\mathbf{x}\|_2}=\max_\mathbf{y} \frac{\|A\mathbf{y}\|_2}{\|V^*\mathbf{y}\|_2}=\max_\mathbf{y} \frac{\|A\mathbf{y}\|_2}{\|\mathbf{y}\|_2}=\|A\|_2.\]

Also, \[\|UAV\|_F=\sqrt{\text{Tr}((UAV)^*UAV)}=\sqrt{\text{Tr}(V^*VU*UA^*A)}=\sqrt{\text{Tr}(A^*A)}=\|A\|_F.\]

Recall that the SVD of \(A\) is \(A=Q_1\Sigma Q_2^*\), where \(Q_1\) and \(Q_2^*\) are unitary, then \(UAV=(UQ_1)\Sigma(Q_2^*V)\), where \(UQ_1\) and \(Q_2^*V\) are unitary. Therefore, \(\sigma_i(A)=\sigma_i(UAV)\), and thus \[\|UAV\|_*=\sum_i \sigma_i(UAV)=\sum_i \sigma_i(A)=\|A\|_*.\]

\(\square\)

Matrix Decomposition

  • Singular value decomposition (SVD): \(A=U\Sigma V^T\).

  • Eigenvalue decomposition: \(A=X\Lambda X^{-1}\), where \(A\) is square. Note that the decomposition usually exists.

    • For normal matrix: \(X\) is unitary.
    • For symmetric matrix: \(X\) is unitary and \(\Lambda\) is real.

  • Jordan decomposition: \(A=XJX^{-1}\), where \(A\) is square and \(J=\text{diag}\left(\begin{bmatrix} \lambda_i & 1 \\ & \lambda_i & \ddots \\ & & \ddots & 1 \\ & & & \lambda_i \end{bmatrix}\right)\)

  • Schur decomposition: \(A=QTQ^*\), where \(Q\) is orthogonal and \(T\) is upper triangular. The eigenvalues of \(A\) is \(\text{diag}(T)\).

  • \(QR\) decomposition: \(A=QR\), where \(Q\) is orthonormal and \(R\) is upper triangular.

  • \(LU\) decomposition: \(A=LU\), where \(L\) is lower triangular and \(U\) is upper triangular.

  • \(SVD\), Schur decomposition and \(QR\) decomposition are orthogonal decompositions, than stable computation is available.
