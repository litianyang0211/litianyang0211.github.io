Courant–Fisher Minmax Theorem

Theorem 1.1 (Courant–Fisher Minmax Theorem)    Suppose \(\mathbf{x} \neq \mathbf{0}\). The \(i\)th largest eigenvalue \(\lambda_i\) of a symmetric matrix \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\) is \[\lambda_i(A)=\max_{\dim \mathcal{S}=i} \min_{\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{S}} \frac{\mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x}}{\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{x}}=\min_{\dim \mathcal{S}=n-i+1} \max_{\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{S}} \frac{\mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x}}{\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{x}}.\] Analogously, for any \(A \in \mathbb{C}^{m \times n}\), where \(m \geq n\), we have \[\sigma_i(A)=\max_{\dim \mathcal{S}=i} \min_{\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{S}} \frac{\|A\mathbf{x}\|_2}{\|\mathbf{x}\|_2}=\min_{\dim \mathcal{S}=n-i+1} \max_{\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{S}} \frac{\|A\mathbf{x}\|_2}{\|\mathbf{x}\|_2}.\]

Proof. We show the case of \(\sigma_i(A)\). Fix an orthonormal \(n \times i\) matrix \(S\) and let \(V_i=\begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{v}_i & \cdots & \mathbf{v}_n \end{bmatrix}\), where \(\mathbf{v}_i\) is the right singular vector of \(A\). We have \(\dim(\mathcal{S})+\dim(\text{Span}(V_i))=i+(n-i+1)=n+1\). Therefore, there exists an intersection \(\mathbf{w} \in S \cap V_i\) s.t. \(\|\mathbf{w}\|_2=1\).

For this \(\mathbf{w}\), we have \[A\mathbf{w}=U\Sigma V^T\mathbf{w}=U\Sigma\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ \vdots \\ 0 \\ c_i \\ \vdots \\ c_n \end{bmatrix}\] and thus \[\|A\mathbf{w}\|_2=\|U\Sigma V^T\mathbf{w}\|_2=\|\Sigma V^T\mathbf{w}\|_2=\|\text{diag}(\sigma_i, \cdots, \sigma_n)V_i^T\mathbf{w}\|_2 \leq \sigma_i.\] Hence, \[\sigma_i(A) \geq \min_{\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{S}}\frac{\|A\mathbf{x}\|_2}{\|\mathbf{x}\|_2}.\]

Taks \(S=\begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{v}_1 & \cdots & \mathbf{v}_i \end{bmatrix}\), where \(\mathbf{w}=\mathbf{v}_i\), then for \(\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{S}\) and \(\|\mathbf{x}\|_2=1\), \[A\mathbf{x}=AS\mathbf{y}=U\Sigma V^TS\mathbf{y}=\widetilde{U}\text{diag}(\sigma_1, \cdots, \sigma_i)\widetilde{\mathbf{y}},\] where \(\|\mathbf{y}\|_2=\|\widetilde{\mathbf{y}}\|_2=1\). Hence, for any \(x \in S\) with \(\|\mathbf{x}\|_2=1\), \(\|A\mathbf{x}\|_2=\|\Sigma_i\widetilde{y}\|_2 \geq \sigma_i(A)\), i.e., \[\min_{\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{S}} \frac{\|A\mathbf{x}\|_2}{\|\mathbf{x}\|_2} \geq \sigma_i(A).\] Therefore, \[\min_{\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{S}} \frac{\|A\mathbf{x}\|_2}{\|\mathbf{x}\|_2}=\sigma_i(A).\] Since we fix an arbitrary subspace \(S\) with \(\dim \mathcal{S}=i\), then we have \[\sigma_i(A)=\max_{\dim \mathcal{S}=i} \min_{\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{S}} \frac{\|A\mathbf{x}\|_2}{\|\mathbf{x}\|_2}.\]

\(\square\)

Weyl's Inequality

Theorem 2.1 (Weyl's Inequality)    For singular values of any matrix \(A\), \[\sigma_i(A+E) \in \sigma_i(A)+[-\|E\|_2, \|E\|_2], \forall i=1, \cdots, n,\] and \[\|A\|_2-\|E\|_2 \leq \|A+E\|_2 \leq \|A\|_2+\|E\|_2.\] For eigenvalues of symmetric matrices \(A\) and \(E\), \[\lambda_i(A+E) \in \lambda_i(A)+[-\|E\|_2, \|E\|_2].\]

Proof. For all \(i=1, \cdots, n\), \[\begin{aligned} \sigma_i(A+E)&=\max_{\dim \mathcal{S}=i} \min_{\substack{\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{S} \\ \|\mathbf{x}\|_2=1}} \|(A+E)\mathbf{x}\|_2 \\&=\max_{\dim \mathcal{S}=i} \min_{\substack{\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{S} \\ \|\mathbf{x}\|_2=1}} \|A\mathbf{x}+E\mathbf{x}\|_2 \\&\leq \max_{\dim \mathcal{S}=i} \min_{\substack{\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{S} \\ \|\mathbf{x}\|_2=1}} (\|A\mathbf{x}\|_2+\|E\mathbf{x}\|_2) \\&\leq \max_{\dim \mathcal{S}=i} \min_{\substack{\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{S} \\ \|\mathbf{x}\|_2=1}} \|A\mathbf{x}\|_2+\|E\|_2 \\&=\sigma_i(A)+\|E\|_2. \end{aligned}\] Besides, we have \(\sigma_i(A)=\sigma_i(A+E+(-E)) \leq \sigma_i(A+E)+\|-E\|_2,\) then \(\sigma_i(A+E) \geq \sigma_i(A)-\|E\|_2\). Therefore, \(\sigma_i(A+E) \in \sigma_i(A)+[-\|E\|_2, \|E\|_2]\).

Let \(i=1\), then it is obvious that \(\|A\|_2-\|E\|_2 \leq \|A+E\|_2 \leq \|A\|_2+\|E\|_2\).

\(\square\)

Singular values and symmetric eigenvalues are insensitive to perturbation (i.e., well-conditioned). Non-symmetric eigenvalues are different.

Eigenvalues of Non-symmetric Matrix

Example 2.1    Consider eigenvalues of a Jordan block and its perturbation \[J=\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ & 1 & \ddots \\ & & \ddots & 1 \\ & & & 1 \end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\] and \[J+E=\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ & 1 & \ddots \\ & & \ddots & 1 \\ \varepsilon & & & 1 \end{bmatrix}.\]

\(\lambda(J)=1\), but \(|\lambda(J+E)-1| \approx \varepsilon^{1/n}\). Hence, the eigenvalues are ill-conditioned.
