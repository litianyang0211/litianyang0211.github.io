Linear System

Posted on 2021-10-26 In Mathematics / Numerical linear algebra
We introduce the LU decomposition, pivoting, and how to solve linear system via Lu decomposition. We also explore Cholesky Factorization for symmetric (semi)positive definite matrix.

LU Decomposition

Let \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\). Suppose we can decompose or factorize \[A=\begin{bmatrix} * & * & * & * & * \\ * & * & * & * & * \\ * & * & * & * & * \\ * & * & * & * & * \\ * & * & * & * & * \end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix} * \\ * & * \\ * & * & * \\ * & * & * & * \\ * & * & * & * & * \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} * & * & * & * & * \\ * & * & * & * \\ * & * & * \\ * & * \\ * \end{bmatrix}=LU,\] where \(L\) is the lower triangular and \(U\) is the upper triangular.

Consider rewriting \(A\) as \[\begin{aligned} A&=\begin{bmatrix} * \\ * \\ * \\ * \\ * \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} * & * & * & * & * \end{bmatrix}+\begin{bmatrix} \\ & * & * & * & * \\ & * & * & * & * \\ & * & * & * & * \\ & * & * & * & * \end{bmatrix} \\&=\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} * \\ * \\ * \\ * \\ * \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} * & * & * & * & * \end{bmatrix}}_{L_1U_1}+\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ * \\ * \\ * \\ * \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 0 & * & * & * & * \end{bmatrix}}_{L_2U_2}+\begin{bmatrix} \\ \\ & & * & * & * \\ & & * & * & * \\ & & * & * & * \end{bmatrix}=\cdots \end{aligned}\]

Specifying the elements, the first step of the algorithm can be described as (\(a=A_{11}\)) \[\begin{aligned} \begin{bmatrix} A_{11} & A_{12} & A_{13} & A_{14} & A_{15} \\ A_{21} \\ A_{31} \\ A_{41} \\ A_{51} \end{bmatrix}&=\begin{bmatrix} L_{11} & & & & \\ L_{21} \\ L_{31} \\ L_{41} \\ L_{51} \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} U_{11} & U_{12} & U_{13} & U_{14} & U_{15} \\ \\ \\ \\ \\ \end{bmatrix}+\begin{bmatrix} \\ & * & * & * & * \\ & * & * & * & * \\ & * & * & * & * \\ & * & * & * & * \end{bmatrix} \\&=\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} 1 & & & & \\ A_{21}/a \\ A_{31}/a \\ A_{41}/a \\ A_{51}/a \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} A_{11} & A_{12} & A_{13} & A_{14} & A_{15} \\ \\ \\ \\ \\ \end{bmatrix}}_{=L_1U_1}+\begin{bmatrix} \\ & * & * & * & * \\ & * & * & * & * \\ & * & * & * & * \\ & * & * & * & * \end{bmatrix} \end{aligned}\]

Assume \(a \neq 0\), repeating the process gives \[\begin{aligned} A&=\begin{bmatrix} * \\ * \\ * \\ * \\ * \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} * & * & * & * & * \end{bmatrix}+\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ * \\ * \\ * \\ * \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 0 & * & * & * & * \end{bmatrix}+\cdots \\&=L_1U_1+L_2U_2+\cdots \\&=\begin{bmatrix} L_1 & L_2 & \cdots \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} U_1 \\ U_2 \\ \vdots \end{bmatrix}=LU. \end{aligned}\]

Solving \(A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}\) via LU

Suppose \(A=LU\), we can efficiently solve an \(n \times n\) linear system \(A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}\): First we solve \(L\mathbf{y}=\mathbf{b}\), then \(U\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{y}\), where \(\mathbf{y}=U\mathbf{x}\). Note that triangular solve is always backward stable.

The computational cost for LU is \(\displaystyle \frac{2}{3}n^2\) flops (floating-point operations), and for the triangular solve is \(\displaystyle \sum_{k=1}^n (2k-1)=\mathcal{O}(n^2)\).

Once we have an LU factorization, we can solve another linear system w.r.t. the same matrix with only \(\mathcal{O}(n^2)\) additional operations.

Pivoting

If \(A_{11}=0\), we need to pivot, i.e., to permute rows s.t. the largest entry of first (active) column is at the top: \(PA=LU\), where \(P\) is a permutation matrix. Note that \(P\) is an orthogonal matrix with only \(0\) and \(1\) entries.

Then \[A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b} \Leftrightarrow PA\mathbf{x}=P\mathbf{b} \Leftrightarrow LU\mathbf{x}=P\mathbf{b}.\] The cost is still \(\displaystyle \frac{2}{3}n^3+\mathcal{O}(n^2)\).

Cholesky Factorization for \(A \succ 0\)

If \(A \succ 0\) (symmetric positive definite or (S)PD), we can take \(U_i=L_i^T=:R_i\) by symmetry, and we do not need to pivot: \[A=\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} * \\ * \\ * \\ * \\ * \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} * & * & * & * & * \end{bmatrix}}_{R_1^TR_1}+\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} \\ & * & * & * & * \\ & * & * & * & * \\ & * & * & * & * \\ & * & * & * & * \end{bmatrix}}_{\text{PSD}}=R^TR.\]

  • The cost for LU is \(\displaystyle \frac{1}{3}n^3\) flops.

  • \(A\) can be written as \(A=R^TR\) for some \(R \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\) iff \(A \succeq 0\).

  • When \(A=A^*\) but \(A\) is not PSD (i.e., negative eigenvalues are present), then \(A=LDL^*\) (LDLT factorization), where \(D\) is diagonal (when \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\), \(D\) can have \(2 \times 2\) diagonal blocks), and \(L\) has \(1\)'s on diagonal.
